What is the history of Ethereum

Ethereum was proposed by Vitalik Buterin in late 2013 and later formally launched on July 30, 2015. Its whitepaper outlined a decentralized platform for building smart contracts and decentralized applications (DApps). The initial coin offering (ICO) held in 2014 raised funds for development. Ethereum's blockchain technology quickly gained traction, attracting developers and entrepreneurs to create a wide range of applications on its platform.

What are the main use cases of Ethereum

Ethereum's versatile blockchain has enabled a myriad of use cases beyond traditional cryptocurrencies. It serves as the foundation for decentralized finance (DeFi) protocols, facilitating lending, borrowing, and yield farming without intermediaries. Additionally, Ethereum's smart contracts are used for crowdfunding (Initial Coin Offerings - ICOs), tokenization of assets, supply chain management, and even powering non-fungible tokens (NFTs) for digital art, collectibles, and gaming.

Who is the founder of Ethereum and what is the total supply of Ether

Ethereum was co-founded by Vitalik Buterin, a prominent figure in the blockchain community. As for the total supply of Ether (ETH), unlike Bitcoin, Ethereum does not have a fixed supply cap. However, it follows a deflationary issuance model through its consensus algorithm, Ethereum 2.0, which aims to reduce issuance over time. As of now, the circulating supply is determined by the rate of new Ether created as block rewards, which can be dynamically adjusted through protocol upgrades.

How does Ethereum's consensus algorithm work

Ethereum's consensus algorithm, currently known as Proof of Stake (PoS), aims to replace its previous Proof of Work (PoW) system. PoS works by validators, also called "stakers," locking up a certain amount of their Ether as a stake in the network. These validators are randomly selected to create new blocks and validate transactions based on their stake's size and time held. Unlike PoW, where miners compete to solve complex puzzles to validate blocks, PoS offers a more energy-efficient and environmentally friendly approach. Validators are incentivized to behave honestly, as they stand to lose their staked Ether if they act maliciously. Ethereum's transition to PoS is part of its Ethereum 2.0 upgrade, aiming to improve scalability, security, and sustainability for the blockchain's future growth.
